Wave Function and Schrodinger Equation

The wave function is a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics that describes the quantum state of a particle or system of particles. Represented typically by the Greek letter Ψ (psi), the wave function contains all the probabilistic information about a system. The square of its magnitude, |Ψ(x,t)|², gives the probability density of finding a particle at position x at time t. Although the wave function itself doesn’t represent a physical observable, it plays a central role in predicting measurable outcomes, such as position, momentum, and energy.

The Schrödinger equation is the key mathematical equation in quantum mechanics that governs how the wave function evolves over time. There are two main forms: the time-dependent Schrödinger equation, which describes how the wave function changes with time, and the time-independent version, used for systems with constant energy. For a single non-relativistic particle in one dimension, the time-dependent equation is written as: iħ ∂Ψ/∂t = - (ħ²/2m) ∂²Ψ/∂x² + V(x)Ψ, where ħ is the reduced Planck’s constant, m is the mass, and V(x) is the potential energy. This equation allows physicists to predict the behavior of quantum systems over time.
